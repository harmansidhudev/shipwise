# Co-founder Agreement Outline

> **Disclaimer: This is an organizational outline, not legal advice. This template provides a framework for discussion — have a qualified startup attorney draft or review your actual agreement before signing.**

## When to use

Use this framework when two or more co-founders are starting a company together. This should be one of the first things you do — ideally before writing any code. The co-founder conversation is uncomfortable, but having it early prevents catastrophic disputes later.

## Decision framework

```
Co-founder Agreement Decisions
│
├── Equity split
│   ├── Equal split? (common for 2 co-founders starting at the same time)
│   │   ├── Pros: Simple, signals trust, no resentment
│   │   └── Cons: May not reflect actual contributions
│   │
│   ├── Contribution-based split?
│   │   ├── Consider: idea origination, domain expertise, full-time vs part-time,
│   │   │   capital invested, network/relationships, opportunity cost
│   │   └── Use a structured framework (see below)
│   │
│   └── Dynamic split (Slicing Pie model)?
│       ├── Equity adjusts based on ongoing contributions
│       ├── Pros: Feels fair, adjusts to reality
│       └── Cons: Complex, harder for investors to understand
│
├── Vesting schedule
│   ├── Standard: 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff
│   │   ├── Year 0-1: Nothing vests (cliff period)
│   │   ├── Year 1: 25% vests on first anniversary
│   │   └── Years 1-4: Remaining 75% vests monthly (2.08%/month)
│   │
│   ├── Acceleration?
│   │   ├── Single-trigger: accelerates on acquisition (founder-friendly)
│   │   ├── Double-trigger: accelerates on acquisition + termination (investor-friendly)
│   │   └── Most VCs will push for double-trigger or no acceleration
│   │
│   └── Vesting start date
│       ├── Company formation date (standard)
│       └── Backdated to when work actually began (if before formation)
│
├── Decision-making authority
│   ├── CEO has final say on day-to-day operations
│   ├── Unanimous required for: fundraising, selling company, taking debt, new co-founders
│   ├── Majority for: hiring/firing, budget > $[CUSTOMIZE], pivots
│   └── Deadlock resolution: mediator, advisor tie-breaker, buyout
│
└── Departure terms
    ├── Voluntary departure ("good leaver")
    │   ├── Keeps vested shares
    │   └── Company has right of first refusal on vested shares at FMV
    │
    ├── Termination for cause ("bad leaver")
    │   ├── Forfeits unvested shares
    │   └── Company can repurchase vested shares at lower of cost or FMV
    │
    └── Death or disability
        ├── Accelerate vesting partially or fully
        └── Life insurance / key-person insurance to fund buyback
```

## Equity split frameworks

### Framework 1: Equal split with vesting

Best for co-founders starting at the same time with comparable contributions. The vesting schedule provides protection — if someone leaves early, they do not walk away with a full share.

### Framework 2: Contribution-based scoring

Rate each founder on these factors (1-10 scale), then weight by importance:

| Factor | Weight | Founder A | Founder B |
|--------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Idea / vision | 5% | | |
| Domain expertise | 15% | | |
| Technical execution | 25% | | |
| Business / sales ability | 15% | | |
| Time commitment (full-time = 10) | 20% | | |
| Capital invested | 10% | | |
| Network / relationships | 5% | | |
| Opportunity cost | 5% | | |
| **Weighted total** | **100%** | | |

Normalize the totals to get each founder's equity percentage.

### Framework 3: Slicing Pie (dynamic equity)

Equity is calculated based on the fair market value of each founder's ongoing contributions (time, money, ideas, relationships, equipment). Equity percentages adjust dynamically until a "freeze event" (funding round, revenue milestone, or agreed date).

- Best for very early stage when contributions are uncertain
- Requires tracking hours and agreed hourly rates
- Book: "Slicing Pie" by Mike Moyer for full methodology

## Customization notes

- **State law matters.** Non-compete enforceability varies dramatically by state. California, Oklahoma, North Dakota, and Minnesota largely prohibit them. Consult a lawyer in your state.
- **83(b) election deadline is absolute.** You must file within 30 days of receiving restricted stock. There are no extensions. Miss it and you face potentially massive tax consequences.
- **Equal splits are not always wrong.** Y Combinator has noted that equal splits among committed co-founders often work well. The key is that all founders are equally committed.
- **Vesting protects everyone.** Even the founder who thinks they will never leave should want vesting — it protects you if your co-founder leaves.
- **Revisit annually.** Roles and responsibilities evolve. Schedule an annual co-founder check-in to review and update the agreement if needed.
- **Divorce is more common than you think.** Over 60% of startups experience co-founder conflict. The agreement is your prenup.
